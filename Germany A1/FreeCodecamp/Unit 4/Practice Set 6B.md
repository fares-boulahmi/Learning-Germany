## Part 1: Target A — "der [ordinal-te/-ste]" vs "am [ordinal-ten/-sten]"

> **The two forms side by side:**
> 
> |Structure|Ending|Example|
> |---|---|---|
> |Ich habe **am** _____ Geburtstag|**-ten / -sten**|am **ersten**, am **dritten**, am **zwanzigsten**, am **dreißigsten**|
> |Heute/Morgen/Gestern ist/war **der** _____|**-te / -ste**|der **erste**, der **dritte**, der **zwanzigste**, der **dreißigste**|
> 
> **Memory rule:** "am" = dative → adds extra -n → **-ten / -sten**. "der" = nominative → NO extra -n → **-te / -ste**.
> 
> The base form is always -te/-ste. "am" is the only context that adds the extra -n.

## Part 2: Target B — Dual-Case Prepositions in Production

> **THE RULE (again, for production):**
> 
> Ask yourself: Is the verb describing WHERE something **IS** (location) or WHERE something is **GOING / BEING PLACED** (movement/action)?
> 
> |Verb type|Case|Example|
> |---|---|---|
> |**IS / liegt / steht / hängt / schläft** (position)|**DATIVE**|Die Katze schläft **unter dem** Tisch|
> |**geht / fährt / legt / stellt / hängt (action) / kommt** (movement/action)|**ACCUSATIVE**|Ich gehe **in die** Küche|
> 
> **The dangerous verbs:** "hängen" means BOTH "to hang (be hanging)" = dative AND "to hang (to put up)" = accusative. Context decides.
> 
> - Das Bild **hängt** an **der** Wand. (it IS hanging there — location — dative)
> - Er **hängt** das Bild an **die** Wand. (he IS HANGING it up — action — accusative)

## Part 3: Target C — Attributive Adjective Endings

> **This is a new topic. Read the explanation carefully before starting.**
> 
> **Two positions for adjectives:**
> 
> 1. **Predicate position** (after sein) → **NO ending**
>     - Das Sofa ist **bequem**. ✓ (no ending after ist)
> 2. **Attributive position** (BEFORE the noun) → **TAKES AN ENDING**
>     - ein **bequemes** Sofa ✓ (ending required before the noun)
>     - ein **bequem** Sofa ✗ (missing ending)
>     - ein **bequemen** Sofa ✗ (wrong ending)
> 
> **The endings depend on the article (ein/eine/einen) and the gender/case:**
> 
> |Case|Masculine|Feminine|Neuter|
> |---|---|---|---|
> |Nominative|ein **alter** Tisch|eine **alte** Kommode|ein **altes** Bett|
> |Accusative|einen **alten** Tisch|eine **alte** Kommode|ein **altes** Bett|
> |Dative|einem **alten** Tisch|einer **alten** Kommode|einem **alten** Bett|
> 
> **Quick rule for "ein" articles:**
> 
> - Nominative masculine: **-er** (ein alter Tisch)
> - Accusative masculine: **-en** (einen alten Tisch)
> - Nominative/Accusative feminine: **-e** (eine alte Kommode)
> - Nominative/Accusative neuter: **-es** (ein altes Bett)
> - ALL dative: **-en** (einem alten Tisch / einer alten Kommode / einem alten Bett)
> 
> **Your specific errors:** "braue Haare" → "braune Haare" (plural accusative), "gelber Kleiderschrank" → "gelben Kleiderschrank" (accusative masculine after einen)

## Part 4: Target D — Plural Dative "den" + Missing "ist"

### Exercise D.1: Plural Dative — Always "den"

> **Rule:** In dative, ALL plural nouns use **"den"** as the article, regardless of gender.
> 
> - die Stühle (plural) → dative → **den** Stühlen _(plural dative adds -n to the noun if it doesn't already end in -n or -s)_
> - die Tische → dative → **den** Tischen
> - die Wände → dative → **den** Wänden
> 
> **Two changes in plural dative:**
> 
> 1. Article: die → **den**
> 2. Noun: often adds **-n** at the end (if not already ending in -n or -s)
